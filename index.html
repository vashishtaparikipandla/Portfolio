The Brief

The client made physical toys for children aged 3 to 12. Good retail presence in the US and UK, a recognisable character universe, but no digital product. Once a toy was bought, the brand disappeared from a family's life completely.

They came to us with one question: can AI storytelling keep families connected to our characters at home? The job was not just to design an app. It was to figure out if the idea was worth building, and what it needed to do to earn a place in a family's regular routine.

"We want parents and kids to experience our characters even after the toy is back on the shelf." (client brief, paraphrased)

Understanding Users

Before anything else I ran two research tracks. A group interview session with 14 parents of children aged 4 to 9 in the US and UK. And a structured review of 200-plus App Store reviews across competitor kids content apps, sorted by complaint theme. No wireframes existed at this point. The goal was to understand how families actually used this kind of product and what got in the way.

What parents feared
78%
of parents said their first concern with any AI product for kids was inappropriate content, before even trying it.
What this told us
Safety was not a feature to design later. It was the first thing a parent needed to feel before they would even give the app a chance.
"I just want to know it won't show my 5-year-old something random."
What parents wanted
11 min
was the total time parents described as ideal for a story session, start to finish, including creating it.
What this told us
Parents wanted a quick, shared activity they could do together at the end of the day. They were not looking for something the child would use alone. Creation had to be fast so the session felt relaxed, not rushed.
"I want something we do together, not something I hand to her and walk away."
What competitors got wrong
6 in 10
App Store reviews of competitor kids apps said it felt designed for the child, not for the parent managing it.
What this told us
Every competitor had designed around the child's experience. Nobody had designed around the parent who sets it up, manages it, and decides whether to keep paying for it.
"It's cute but honestly I have no idea what my son is looking at half the time."

What the Research Changed

Three things came out of research that shifted the entire direction before a single wireframe was drawn.

1. The product is for parents, not kids

The brief said "an AI app for kids." Research said the parent is the one who downloads it, sets it up, manages the content, and decides whether to pay. Children are the audience, not the user. This reframe changed the safety model, the onboarding, and when we ask for money. I brought this back to the client in the week 2 readout and we agreed to redesign the brief before going any further.

Original brief

"An AI app for kids"

  • Kids are not the paying customer
  • Moderation and safety responsibility is huge
  • Competes with free YouTube and games
  • No natural connection to toy sales
Reframed after research

"A parent-led story session for families with kids aged 4 to 9"

  • Parent is buyer, account holder, and main user
  • Fits into family story time naturally
  • Subscription and toy cross-sell both make sense
  • Safety model is clear: parent creates, child listens

2. The business model had to be defined before design started

Once we knew parents were the real user, the monetisation approach became clearer. I pushed for this to be agreed with the client before wireframes, not after. Two streams made sense.

Freemium subscription

Free tier with 5 stories per month. Premium with unlimited stories, narration, voice packs, and branded story packs from the client's toy line. $4.99 per month or $39 per year.

In-app toy discovery

When a story features a character from the client's range, the app surfaces "Get the toy" at story end. The story becomes the discovery channel for physical product sales. This was the main business case for the client beyond subscriptions.

Distribution was tied to physical product from the start: QR codes inside toy packaging, retail store promotions, and parenting blog partnerships. Every toy sold in a store was a potential app download.

3. Creation time had to be a design constraint, not a nice-to-have

Parents said 11 minutes was ideal for the full session. That meant story creation could take at most 2 minutes, leaving the rest for listening together. We set 90 seconds as a hard target and tested against it through every prototype round. This became one of the main constraints that shaped the builder flow.

How Might We…

  • 1How do we make story creation fast enough to fit inside a family story session without it feeling like a chore?
  • 2How do we show parents that the content is safe before asking them to sign up?
  • 3How do we surface the physical toy at the right moment so it feels like a recommendation, not an ad?
  • 4How do we time the subscription ask so it lands when the parent is most satisfied with the product?
  • 5How do we build a feedback loop so the AI output gets better the more a family uses it?

Scoping Phase 1

After research readout I ran two FigJam sessions with the product and client team. The first was a full open brainstorm: AR toy scanning, voice cloning, social sharing, peer-to-peer story gifting, AI chapter sequels, all ideas welcome. The second was a prioritisation using an effort-versus-impact grid.

We cut AR scanning (high effort, value unproven), social sharing (moderation risk too early in the product's life), and parent-voice narration (moved to Phase 2 as a premium anchor feature). What stayed was the core ritual and we committed to doing it properly.

Design Decisions and What We Learned

78% of parents, safety was the first concern

1. Safety had to come before features

Research showed parents would not give the app a proper chance until they felt the content was safe. So we put safety communication on screen 2 of onboarding, before asking for any child information. Age guidance, content controls, and the parent-managed model in plain language. Parents who saw this early completed onboarding at a much higher rate compared to a version where safety came after sign-up.

88 seconds average creation time, target was 90

2. Guided choices, not an open text box

Early prototype testing showed that the moment parents faced a blank text prompt, they slowed down or stopped. We replaced it with three visual steps: theme cards, personalisation inputs (child name, character pick, setting), and a single generate button. The AI used these structured inputs as its prompt. Average creation time in testing was 88 seconds. The first line of every story always used the child's name, which every parent in testing flagged as the moment the product clicked for them.

3.1x conversion, post-save prompt vs. onboarding prompt

3. Ask for money after the product has earned it

We A/B tested the subscribe prompt at two moments across prototype sessions: right after onboarding, and right after a parent saved their first story. Post-save converted at 3.1x the rate. By that point the product had worked, the child had reacted well, and the parent was satisfied. That was the right moment. We also kept the free story counter visible throughout so parents were never surprised when they hit the monthly limit.

3.4x more toy page visits vs. client social media in the pilot

4. "Get the toy from tonight's story" felt like a suggestion, not an ad

We tested three placements for the toy card: mid-story (immediately disruptive, removed), just after the story ended before the save prompt (clashed), and after save confirmation. The third placement worked. The parent was already in a positive state. Copy mattered too: "Get the toy from tonight's story" with the featured character felt like a natural follow-up. Pilot data showed toy page visits from in-app referrals were 3.4x more than from the client's own social campaigns. This became the main argument for continued investment in the product.

74% of pilot stories rated 4 or 5 stars

5. The story rating was training signal, not just a satisfaction score

I designed the post-story rating as structured feedback for the model team, not just a UX metric. Every story log captured theme, inputs, and generation settings alongside the rating. Stories rated below 3 stars from the same input pattern were reviewed weekly. Safety reports went for immediate review. A parent who notices stories getting noticeably better over a few weeks is far more likely to stay subscribed. This was the argument for building the feedback loop in Phase 1, not leaving it for later.

Background

In 2024, the client had already built a working image-generation demo. It was rough, but the output quality stood out. In internal comparisons against image-generation tools of that period, the model performed especially well on one capability that mattered for consumer adoption: preserving a user’s facial identity across stylised scene transformations.

That mattered because many image tools at the time could create flashy outputs, but they often changed the person too much. Faces drifted, features blurred, and the result looked like “someone inspired by you” instead of actually feeling like you. This model was much better at what the broader space often described as identity consistency or identity-preserving generation.

The product opportunity was clear: if the model could reliably keep people looking like themselves, the UX had to help them trust that promise and get to it quickly.

What made the technology different

The client’s internal team had benchmarked the demo against several image-generation patterns common in 2024: open prompt-based creation, generic avatar generation, and style-transfer-like transformations. While it was not the best model for broad artistic freedom, it was meaningfully stronger at maintaining identity cues such as face shape, eye placement, and recognisable likeness after transformation.

That gave us a sharper product angle. We were not designing a general image toy. We were designing around a specific strength: face-consistent scene generation for consumers who wanted to see themselves inside the result.

The product problem

The internal demo proved the model worked, but it was not yet a product. There was no clear browse layer, no onboarding, no strong expectation-setting around photo quality, and no thoughtful path from first success to paid value.

I reframed the challenge around three simple questions: how do we make first use feel easy, how do we make AI output feel trustworthy, and how do we turn one good result into repeat behaviour?

Research inputs

Because this was 0-to-1 product work, the research model was intentionally light but useful. I reviewed competing AI image apps, mapped their first-session journeys, and looked at where users were likely to hesitate: choosing a style, uploading a usable image, judging whether the output was “good enough,” and deciding if the app was worth paying for.

For the expected launch market, we designed around mobile-first users in urban European markets, with early adoption likely among younger adults, creators, and social-first users looking for quick self-expression. That made speed, clarity, and visual confidence more important than feature depth on day one.

Strategy

The most important call I made was to move away from prompt-first interaction and toward scene-led selection. On mobile, asking users to imagine and write the right prompt creates friction. Giving them strong visual scenes reduces blank-state anxiety and gets them to value faster.

From there, I built the journey around four clear stages: browse, choose, generate, and act. That gave the app a usable product loop instead of a one-time generation mechanic.

Key flows

Browse and scene discovery

I designed the home experience to explain the product visually. Categories like sports, fantasy, and historical moments made the model’s capabilities legible without forcing users to understand technical AI concepts.

Lightweight social proof like likes and saves also helped users feel that certain scenes were safer bets for a good first outcome.

Guided generation

Once a scene was selected, the app narrowed the task to a simple upload-and-generate flow. I kept the chosen scene visible throughout so people always knew what they were making and what kind of result to expect.

I also designed this flow to quietly educate users. Better source images create better AI outputs, so the UX had to guide people without making the process feel technical or demanding.

Result evaluation

The result screen was designed as a trust layer, not just a celebration state. Users could compare, save, share, regenerate, or discard the image, which helped the output feel reviewable rather than final.

This mattered because AI products earn trust when users feel they still have judgement and control after generation.

Premium flow

The premium flow was positioned after successful outcomes, not before them. I framed it around simple benefits users could understand: more generations, cleaner exports, faster processing, and higher-quality results.

That timing helped paid conversion settle at 4.8% in the first 90 days, which was healthy for a new AI image app in an early consumer market.

AI UX principles we followed

AI pattern 1

Be clear that AI may vary

We treated every result as generated content, not guaranteed truth. The product needed visible language that AI outputs can vary and may not always match expectation exactly.

AI pattern 2

Set expectations before input

We used guidance before upload because AI quality depends on source quality. Good AI UX starts by shaping the input, not just reacting to bad output later.

AI pattern 3

Give users control after generation

Save, delete, regenerate, and compare actions help users feel that they are still directing the experience rather than accepting a black-box answer.

AI pattern 4

Disclose synthetic media honestly

Wherever images are exported or shared, the experience should support clear AI-generated disclosure and avoid presenting machine-generated media as candid photography.

Suggested AI-specific copy and safeguards

  • Upload hint: Use a clear front-facing photo for best results. Generated images may vary based on image quality and scene choice.
  • Result disclaimer: This image was generated using AI and may contain visual inconsistencies.
  • Share/export note: AI-generated image. not a real photograph.
  • Privacy note: Ask permission before using another person’s face image and explain how uploaded photos are used.

What this project shows

This case matters because it shows how I work with emerging technology. I can look at a model demo, identify the real consumer value inside it, and design the product layer that makes that value understandable and monetisable.

It also shows that I think beyond the happy path. In AI products, trust, disclosure, and user control are not side details. They are part of the core UX.

The Starting Point

When I joined BeyondTheLoop, there was a partial Figma file functioning as a "design system" in the sense that it existed. Components had been created as needed. a button here, a modal there. with no naming convention, no token layer, and no documentation. Engineering had their own interpretations built sprint by sprint without a reference to check against.

The result was a product that looked almost right in most places, and slightly off in all of them. Inconsistent border radii on similar cards. Three different shades of the same "muted text" colour across screens. Buttons with the same visual intent but different sizes. None of it was broken. All of it added up to a product that felt unfinished.

The problem wasn't design quality. It was design infrastructure. There was nothing to be consistent with.

The Brief I Set for Myself

This wasn't a client brief. it was a problem I identified in week one and proposed adding to scope. Three constraints shaped every decision:

  • Buildable in parallel. Every new component I designed for a feature became a library component at the same time. No separate "system sprint."
  • Useful without documentation. The founders were moving fast. The system had to be self-explanatory through naming and organisation alone.
  • Token-first, not component-first. If the token layer was wrong, every component built on it would be wrong too. The sequence mattered.

  • 2How might we build a system that scales from a team of one to a team of ten without a full redesign?
  • 3How might we eliminate "which version is correct?" as a category of question entirely?

The Architecture. Tokens First

I started with the token layer before touching any component. Tokens are the vocabulary; components are the sentences. Getting the vocabulary wrong means rewriting every sentence later.

Tier 1. Primitive tokens

The raw values. Every colour, every spacing unit, every radius, every type size. Named by what they are, not what they do. color-gray-600, not color-text-secondary. These never get used directly in components. they're the foundation for the semantic layer above.

Tier 2. Semantic tokens

The intent layer. color-text-primary maps to color-gray-900 in light mode, color-gray-100 in dark. This is the layer components reference. When dark mode needed to ship, zero component values changed. only the semantic token mappings flipped.

Tier 3. Component tokens

For components with enough variation to warrant their own namespace. button-primary-bg, button-primary-bg-hover. Engineering could implement a global button change by updating two token values instead of hunting across multiple files.

The Component Library. 24 Components in 8 Weeks

Built in parallel with feature design. Every component I designed for a feature screen was immediately standardised, named, and added to the library. 24 components across 6 categories, all interactive states documented.

Foundations

Colour system (primitive + semantic tokens), type scale (5 sizes, 3 weights), spacing scale (4px base unit), elevation (3 shadow levels), border radius (4 values), iconography guidelines using the Lucide icon set.

Actions

Primary, secondary, ghost, icon, and destructive buttons. all with default, hover, active, focus, disabled, and loading states. One naming convention across all: [component]-[variant]-[state]. Engineering stopped asking which button to use for which context.

Inputs

Text input, textarea, select dropdown, checkbox, radio, toggle. all states documented. The model selector used the select component. Magic Write used the textarea. Both pulled from the same library, same tokens, zero drift between them.

Feedback

Toast notifications (success, warning, error, info), inline validation messages, empty states, loading skeletons. The empty state component was the one engineering thanked me for most. they'd been designing empty states from scratch every sprint.

Navigation

Top nav bar, sidebar, tab set, breadcrumb. Any future navigation change is now a component update, not a full redesign.

Data display

Stat card (admin dashboard), data table with sorting and pagination, model identity pill (built for CS2), usage bar chart. The admin dashboard shipped in 4 days partly because every component it needed already existed.

The Decisions That Made It Actually Get Used

Built as I shipped, not before I shipped

Every component I designed for a feature became a library component on the same day. The library was always immediately useful. it contained exactly the things the team was actively building. There was no system sprint that delayed feature work.

Named for engineers, not designers

Component and token names matched what an engineer would naturally type. button-primary not CTA Button - Brand. Figma layer names and CSS variable names were identical wherever possible. Zero translation layer between design and code.

Dark mode baked in from day one

Dark mode was a roadmap item, not a current requirement. I built it into the token architecture anyway. semantic tokens with both light and dark mappings. When engineering was ready, it was a CSS variable swap, not a redesign. This looks like over-engineering until the moment someone asks for it.

What I'd Do Differently

I built the library entirely in Figma. Engineering implemented it in code independently, using the Figma file as reference. The two stayed in sync only because I was active enough to maintain them manually. If I were doing this again, I'd push the token layer into a shared JSON file (via Tokens Studio) that both Figma and the codebase pull from. One source of truth, not two files that happen to agree.

What This Was Really About

Design systems are often presented as a gift from design to engineering. This one was built as an honest acknowledgement that the way we'd been working wasn't sustainable. The brief

Strata had been operating in India for over a decade - soil reinforcement, geotechnical solutions, infrastructure projects across the country. Real expertise, real track record. But their digital presence was a patchwork: separate websites for different regions, each with different structures, different depths of content, and different levels of polish. For a senior engineer evaluating vendors from a desktop in Singapore or Houston, it felt like arriving at a company that hadn't quite decided what it wanted to be globally.

The support team was fielding calls about things that should have been self-serve - case studies, product specs, technical downloads. Leads were coming in vague because the site wasn't giving people enough to make a specific ask.

One platform. Global audience. Make it obvious - within a few clicks - that Strata knows what it's doing and has done it before.

The team

I worked with Anisha Agarwal (UX Designer), Vikissha Mehta (Project Manager), Komal Angane (Content), and Anil Yadav (Engineering). We ran the project using a Double Diamond approach - broad discovery first, then a tight define-develop-deliver loop. Seven weeks, research through design.

One early call that changed the project

We kicked off with a mobile-first assumption. Standard practice, reasonable default. Then we started talking to users.

The primary audience for a B2B geotechnical platform isn't someone browsing on a train. It's a senior civil engineer doing a detailed vendor evaluation at their desk - looking at technical drawings, cross-referencing case studies, downloading spec sheets. They need density and scannability, not a stripped-down mobile flow. After a round of stakeholder interviews and user sessions, we made the call: web-first, with mobile as a supporting experience, not the lead one.

The device data backed it up. But honestly, five minutes talking to the actual users made it obvious. Nobody was doing a vendor evaluation for a soil reinforcement contractor on their phone.

What we found - the audit

Before talking to anyone, we did a heuristic evaluation of the existing sites. Every major page, every key flow - categorised across navigation, discoverability, consistency, engagement, and content structure.

The consistent themes:

  • No guided navigation - users landed on pages with no clear sense of what to do next
  • Information architecture that reflected Strata's internal structure, not how users actually look for things
  • Key content - case studies, product applications, certifications - buried or completely undiscoverable
  • Visual inconsistency across pages, and a brand colour that failed basic WCAG contrast checks
  • No search, no thought leadership, no projection of technical expertise
  • Content written for someone who already knew what Strata did - not someone trying to find out

Research - what stakeholders and users actually needed

We ran two tracks of interviews in parallel. On the business side: Heads of Business, Technology, Brand, Sales, and Marketing. We needed to understand the product range, the commercial goals, the brand positioning, and what they expected from the redesign. On the user side: current customers, prospective customers, and civil engineers across different specialisations.

The business side wanted three things: reduce inbound support calls about basic information, capture better-qualified leads (specifically from the US market), and build something they could update dynamically without calling an agency every time.

Users wanted something simpler: show me you've done this before, let me find the technical data I need, and connect me to the right person without making me work for it.

Who we were designing for

The stakeholder interviews surfaced seven distinct user groups, each arriving at the site with a different question and needing different information to answer it.

  • Distributors - looking for partnership credibility: client lists, case studies, product specs, contact routes
  • Contractors - looking for proof of relevant site experience and consultancy capability
  • Engineers - needing technical downloads, product application data, and problem-solving references
  • Architects & Consultants - recognition, case studies, services, testimonials
  • Corporate Partners - internal stakeholders wanting global presence overview and company updates
  • Job Seekers - careers, culture, team, and company history
  • Media - press releases, project showcases, expert access, media kit

These user groups became the backbone of the information architecture. Every nav decision, every page template, every content priority was run against the question: which user groups does this serve, and what does it need to do for them?

Restructuring the information architecture

We built a comprehensive site map covering all pages, their priorities, their connections, and the gaps. Then we validated it - card sorting with both business stakeholders and external users - to make sure the structure made sense to the people navigating it, not just the people who built it.

Content flow - designing for different levels of knowledge

A big part of this project was figuring out how to handle the range of content without overwhelming people. Strata's audience spans someone who has never heard of geosynthetics to a specialist engineer who knows the product range inside out. We worked with the content team to segment users by domain knowledge and company familiarity, then built content flows for each page around that segmentation - what gets surfaced upfront, what lives deeper, where CTAs sit.

This also shaped the UX writing: language and tone matched to where a user was in their understanding, not a single register trying to serve everyone at once.

Wireframes, and a deliberate shortcut

We prototyped in Adobe XD, building out navigation, content, and interactions at low fidelity first. The original plan was to test wireframes with 15–20 users before moving to high-fidelity. We didn't. Time was tight, the content team had made significant progress on revised copy, and testing with real copy in place meant we could validate structure and language together - which gave us more useful feedback than testing structural skeletons alone.

We tested the high-fidelity prototypes with five participants from each primary user group - the same people we'd interviewed in research. They already had a mental model of the old site, which gave us meaningful before-and-after signal.

Three decisions worth naming

Decision 1 · Navigation

Anchor links for overview and direct navigation

Product pages on the old site were long and undifferentiated - you had to scroll through everything to find anything. We restructured each page with an overview section at the top and anchor links directly into the relevant content. Engineers looking for technical drawings didn't have to read the marketing intro first. Contractors looking for case studies could jump straight there. One structural change that worked for almost every user group.

Decision 2 · Lead capture

CAD drawings visible upfront, behind a pre-filled form

Engineers were calling support to ask for technical drawings that already existed on the site - they just weren't findable. We made the full library of CAD drawings visible upfront on product pages. Accessing a drawing opened a pre-selected request form, pre-filtered to the relevant product. Business got qualified leads. Users got the data they needed without a phone call. The same pattern applied to consultation: free consultancy was a Strata differentiator that barely featured on the old site. We built it into every product page as a clear CTA with a pre-filtered enquiry form behind it.

Decision 3 · Accessibility

Negotiating brand colour to meet WCAG

The legacy brand colour failed basic WCAG contrast checks - not edge cases, fundamental contrast ratios on body text and navigation. We went to the client's branding team with a proposal: alternate hues of the same colour family that preserved the brand feel but met the requirements. They picked the second option we suggested. The site launched fully compliant without looking like a different company. We raised it rather than quietly shipping something inaccessible. That was the right call.

Visual design

The industry context shaped a lot of the visual decisions. Geotechnical and construction is a sector that values substance over style - competitors are functional, traditional, content-heavy. There's room for modern typography and image-led design, but stakeholders and users both responded better to something authoritative and familiar rather than design-forward. The final visual language landed in the middle: cleaner than the old site, more contemporary, but not so far from the industry norm that it felt like a different kind of company. That constraint produced better decisions than a blank brief would have.

Handoff and delivery

We worked closely with the engineering team through the build - cross-checking implementations against final designs, giving feedback as pages went live. The project hit friction in the development phase and I ended up taking on responsibilities outside my original scope to keep it moving. That's not always the plan. But it's the right call when the alternative is watching the timeline slip.

This project ran during the first months of the pandemic. Every interview, workshop, stakeholder review, and usability test happened remotely. You adapt. But looking back, it genuinely shaped how I work - virtual research requires more structure, not less. You lose the ambient signal you get in a room with someone, so your questions have to be sharper.

The bigger lesson was about industry-specific design. I came in thinking there was a clear opportunity to push the visual language further. The research told a different story. In a high-stakes B2B context, a site that looks too designed can actually raise questions about whether it's the right kind of company. Working within that constraint produced better design decisions than a blank brief would have.

The brief

Fearless Pharma had a hardware problem that was also a data problem. Their sanitiser dispensers were installed across hospitals and food industry facilities. but they were dumb. Staff refilled them on a fixed schedule, not based on actual usage. The result was predictable: dispensers running dry in high-traffic areas, or being refilled when they were still half-full. Neither outcome was acceptable in a healthcare environment.

The goal was to design a connected dispenser and an accompanying dashboard that gave facility managers real-time visibility. and gave them something to act on, not just look at.

Who we were designing for

Two users, one system, very different jobs to do.

The facility manager needed to see everything. usage patterns, refill alerts, dispenser status across multiple floors. without having to walk the building or chase staff. They wanted one screen that told them whether things were okay or needed attention right now.

The hospital staff and cleaning crew needed the dispenser itself to be frictionless. They weren't sitting at dashboards. They were moving fast, managing a lot, and the dispenser had to work the first time, every time. with no learning curve and clear feedback about when it was running low.

A third stakeholder shaped the constraints: hospital administrators who cared about compliance data, and procurement teams who needed usage reports for budgeting decisions.

The dispenser isn't a product people choose to interact with. It's infrastructure. Which means any friction. any moment where it doesn't behave as expected. registers as a failure, not an inconvenience.

What the research told us

We ran two tracks in parallel. ethnographic observation and stakeholder interviews.

For the observation work, we visited a hospital branch in Navi Mumbai, watching how current sanitisation processes actually played out: where dispensers sat, how often staff checked them, how refills got triggered. What we found was almost entirely reactive. There was no signal until something was already wrong.

The interviews covered hospital administrators, food industry managers, and cleaning staff. A few things came up consistently:

  • Nobody had real-time visibility. Refill schedules were based on estimates, not data.
  • Compliance reporting was manual and time-consuming. Staff were filling out paper logs that no one was acting on.
  • In high-traffic zones. entrances, ICU corridors, canteens. usage spikes were predictable but completely unaccounted for.
  • Trust in the data mattered as much as the data itself. If a reading seemed off, staff would default to visual checks anyway.

That last point shaped a lot of the dashboard design. The system needed to be accurate enough. and transparent enough about how it worked. that people would stop second-guessing it.

What we were actually solving

From the research, four design areas emerged clearly:

Refill management

Surface the right alert at the right time

Not a low-battery-style warning when it's already too late. Managers needed to see trend data, not just current state. so they could get ahead of a dry dispenser, not respond to one.

Usage analytics

Make patterns visible and actionable

Usage varied by floor, time of day, and day of week. The dashboard needed to make that visible in a way that informed staffing and procurement decisions, not just create more noise in the interface.

Compliance and reporting

The data was there. Nobody was surfacing it.

Compliance reports that took two hours to compile manually should take two minutes to export. That was the bar.

Device feedback

Communicate status without requiring attention

For the dispenser hardware itself, the interface had to communicate status from across a corridor. A LED ring that reads as "full," "half," or "low" at a glance. No reading required.

Three design decisions worth naming

01. Predictive refill over reactive alert

The first version of the alert logic was threshold-based. when level drops below 20%, send a notification. That's still reactive. We redesigned around usage rate: if a dispenser in ICU-B is depleting at 3× normal speed, the alert fires before it hits the threshold. The manager gets ahead of it, not behind it.

02. Floor-level view before unit-level view

Early explorations showed the whole dispenser inventory in a list. Facility managers don't think in unit IDs. They think in floors and zones. We restructured the dashboard hierarchy to match their mental model. start with the floor, drill into zones, then individual dispensers. Sorting by "needs attention" surfaced the right units first.

03. Export as a first-class feature, not an afterthought

Compliance reporting was an afterthought in the first dashboard wireframes. a small link tucked in the nav. After two rounds of stakeholder interviews, it moved to the primary toolbar. For administrators, that was the whole point of having the data. It needed to feel like a real workflow, not a data dump.

What the device needed to do

The dispenser interface had almost no real estate to work with. The key decisions:

  • A full-ring LED indicator visible from a distance. green, amber, red. no text required
  • A single press to dispense, no sensor calibration required from users after setup
  • An audible low-level alert that triggered only during off-hours, so it didn't disrupt patient environments during the day
  • All pairing and configuration handled by the admin app at setup. zero interaction required from staff after that

The constraint made the design better. When there's nothing to remove, you're forced to make every decision count.

What I took from it

This was an earlier project in my portfolio, and it taught me something I've carried into every IoT or multi-touchpoint problem since: the hardware and software aren't separate design problems. The quality of the data the dispenser sends upstream determines what the dashboard can say. And what the dashboard can say determines whether anyone trusts it enough to act on it. They're one system, and they have to be designed that way.

The medical context also raised the stakes on every decision. In a sector where "it worked most of the time" isn't good enough, edge cases and failure states aren't nice-to-haves. They're the design.

Design decisions

The calls that defined Phase 1.

Decision 01

Feed as the default home, not a tab

We could have added Feed as a new tab inside Connect's existing navigation. I recommended against it. Making Feed the default opening surface was the signal that Connect was evolving as a platform. Research showed that employees opened Connect an average of 4.2 times per day, but always directly to a specific tool. A default feed placement would intercept those sessions and build habitual exposure to comms content without requiring employees to change anything. The competitor study backed this: top-of-hierarchy placement drives habitual return on Workplace and LinkedIn for exactly this reason.

Decision 02

Role-based filters, not an algorithm

For Phase 1, I chose explicit filter controls by content type and employee role over any ranking model. We had no behavioural baseline to train one. More importantly, 73% of survey respondents said they would trust a channel more if they understood what it showed them and why. Transparent filtering is legible. An algorithm in a first-time channel is a black box. Trust first, personalisation in Phase 2.

Decision 03

Feed consistency over formatting freedom

Early prototypes let publishers embed rich formatting directly in the post card. The feed immediately looked chaotic. Posts of wildly different visual weight destroyed the hierarchy and made the whole thing feel untrustworthy. I moved to an article-first model where rich content lives inside a structured post format, and the feed itself stays consistent and scannable. That argument had to be made with prototypes in the room, not in a document.

Decision 04

Search that works across tools and resources, not just one

Before this, search in Connect only surfaced tools by default. An employee looking for a benefits update had to know to change the dropdown, and almost nobody did. The redesigned search, powered by BlueLine trained on ServiceNow data, works across both. Best Buy · Contextual Nudges

The situation

Push notifications are one of Best Buy's main levers for deals, offers, and service updates - directly tied to revenue and retention. By the end of 2024, opt-in rate was at 43%. The FY2025 target was 50%. Not a massive gap on paper, but 7 percentage points across a user base of that size is a lot of people.

The app already had nudges in 7 places. Only three of them - positions 1, 3, and 6 - were driving almost all of the opt-ins. Previous attempts to improve the other placements had consistently failed. The brief from Product: find new, high-intent, contextual moments to ask - especially for non-member users - without compromising privacy or user trust.

My role was end-to-end: framing the problem, leading discovery, defining the nudge strategy, and designing the experience. Product and Analytics were in the room for data decisions and feasibility, but the UX direction was mine to own.

Why non-members

The baseline data revealed a clear segmentation gap. Members opted in at roughly 3x the rate of non-members. But this wasn't a user demand problem - it was a design and messaging problem. Members had already been primed through loyalty context and prior app engagement. Non-members were getting the same generic nudge with zero personal relevance.

Non-members were also the bigger opportunity by far: they represented around 70% of the eligible user base but only 13% of opt-ins. A focused 10-point lift in non-member opt-in rate - from 27% to 37% - would alone contribute 5 to 6 points toward the 50% target. That's where we focused.

An in-app survey of non-members added another layer. When asked what types of notifications they'd actually want:

  • Saved Items Price Drop Notifications - 23%
  • Flash Sale or Limited-Time Offer Alerts - 21%
  • Gift Recommendations - 20.5%
  • Customer Service Updates - 18%
  • Personalised Shopping Suggestions - 7%
  • New Product Launch Alerts - 5%

Price drops and sale alerts were clearly what non-members cared about. That shaped the copy strategy across every nudge we designed - lead with the specific value, not a generic "allow notifications" ask.

The Saved Items story

This is the one I want to spend the most time on - not because it worked, but because of what happened when it didn't.

The "Saved Items & Lists" page looked like a promising nudge location. Users had already shown intent by saving something. Our survey data backed it up - price drop alerts were the top thing non-members said they'd want. On the surface, the case seemed obvious.

But when I looked at the actual conversion data, one number stood out: only 0.6% of users who added items to their cart from the Saved Items page went on to purchase. That's not a healthy funnel. Adding a nudge that encouraged users to hold off and wait for a price drop - which is what Product was proposing - risked making that number worse, not better.

My concern with the product team's proposal

The proposal was to show a nudge when users landed on the Saved Items page, prompting them to enable price drop alerts instead of removing items from their list. The logic made sense at a surface level. But I was worried we'd be actively interrupting users at a moment of purchase consideration and redirecting their attention toward a future event - which, given the existing 0.6% conversion rate, felt like pouring more water into a leaking bucket.

I pushed back. Not against nudging on this page entirely, but against nudging at the wrong moment within it.

What I proposed instead

Two more contextual triggers - both of which respond to something the user is actually doing rather than just landing on a page:

  • Nudge when the user clicks "Edit" or tries to remove an item - that's the moment of highest relevance. "Don't remove this - we'll let you know if the price drops." The user is about to lose the item. The value of the notification is immediately obvious.
  • Nudge when the user tries to exit the page - a soft exit-intent prompt, not an aggressive modal. "Your saved items will still be here. We'll notify you if anything changes in price." Low interruption, high relevance.

What actually shipped - and what happened

Despite the data and the pushback, Product moved forward with the on-load nudge on the Saved Items page. Their reasoning had more to do with roadmap momentum than user behaviour. The nudge went live.

By the end of Q1 2025, it had added roughly 5,000 new opt-ins - about 0.1%. And the 0.6% purchase conversion rate had dropped to 0.3%. Exactly what we were worried about. The business team pulled the nudge.

We're now pushing for the edit-intent and exit-intent patterns to be added back to the roadmap. They haven't shipped yet.

PDP to GHP - what went live and worked

While exploring the Product Detail Page, we found a behaviour pattern worth acting on. After a user saves a product on the PDP and exits to the homepage, they're essentially done exploring for now - the homepage is their resting point. We confirmed this with CJA data: around 2 lakh clicks on the PDP-to-home path were ending the session, not continuing it.

That exit moment turned out to be a good place to ask. The user had just expressed clear intent - saved a product - and was pausing. A bottom drawer nudge offering deal alerts for that specific product felt timely, not intrusive. We A/B tested two variants: one with a "Not now" button, one with just a close icon in the corner. The close icon variant performed better - around 4% increase in overall opt-ins, adding roughly 3,000 new users in the first month. This one is live.

One thing we learned during testing: animated nudges, while visually interesting, added cognitive load at the wrong moment. A user who hasn't opted in yet is already evaluating whether to trust the ask - layering an animation on top of that made them hesitate, not engage. We kept the final design clean and static.

Sale event nudges - a glimpse

Seasonal sale events - Black Friday, Back to School, Christmas - see some of the highest app traffic of the year, with a significant portion of those users having push notifications disabled. We worked on a GHP-level nudge strategy for these events: a global opt-in for sale alerts plus a product-specific SKU carousel for users who wanted to track specific items. Both global and product-specific approaches were needed because a global nudge alone would miss users who didn't see anything they wanted, and a product-specific one would miss casual browsers. This is still in progress.

What I took away

The Saved Items story is the most useful thing in this case study - not because it's a win, but because it shows what happens when design intuition backed by data gets overruled by roadmap pressure. The nudge failed in exactly the way we said it would. That's not a satisfying outcome, but it's an honest one.

The bigger lesson: document everything when you have a clear, data-backed position. Not to say "I told you so" later - but because a documented hypothesis gives you the foundation to re-enter the conversation when things go wrong. That's how the edit-intent pattern is getting back on the roadmap now.

Why this project

In August 2023, India passed the Digital Personal Data Protection Act - a defining law that puts real accountability on companies around how they collect, store, and delete personal data. Unlike GDPR or CCPA, the DPDP Act is designed for India's scale: hundreds of millions of mobile-first users with widely varying digital literacy levels. The law isn't just asking for better privacy policies. It's asking for tools that make compliance transparent and usable on both sides - for the person whose data it is, and for the company responsible for it.

When MeitY announced a design challenge around the Act, Phence Private Limited asked me to lead the design of Privasync - a two-sided consent management platform responding directly to what the law requires.

This project was close to me personally. Privacy and data control are things I care about. Getting a chance to design a product that treated consent as an experience rather than a checkbox felt worth doing properly.

What Privasync needed to be

The law created two distinct users who needed completely different products:

  • Data Principals - everyday citizens who want to see which companies have their data, manage their consent, and raise access or deletion requests without needing a law degree to do it
  • Chief Compliance Officers (CCOs) and privacy teams - company-side roles responsible for receiving and responding to those requests, building automated workflows, and demonstrating compliance to regulators

The key highlights of the Act that shaped almost every design decision: informed consent is mandatory before any personal data is processed; every Data Principal has the right to access, correct, delete, or restrict their data; companies must respond to requests within defined timelines; non-compliance can lead to fines of up to ₹250 crore or more.

Who helped shape it

I wasn't working alone. Varun Thopucherla (CEO) and Amarnath (CTO) at Phence gave me consistent feedback from a non-designer, operations, and technical implementation lens. They helped validate user flows, simplify things I'd over-engineered, and keep the design grounded in what could actually be built. That kind of input - from people who think about systems rather than screens - made a real difference to the quality of what we produced.

Research and personas

I started by looking at existing tools - Transcend.io, Ketch - and they were well-made but built for GDPR or CCPA. The assumptions baked into them didn't translate to India's context: the language, the scale, the mobile-first reality, the varying literacy levels. So I set those aside and mapped from the brief outward.

Two personas, built from the legal requirements and validated with the Phence team. I used them actively throughout - not just as research artefacts, but as prompts when working with AI tools. When I needed to write consent summary screens or adjust tone on legal copy, I framed the prompt around the persona first.

How AI changed my process

This was the first project where I leaned heavily into ChatGPT and Google Stitch - not to replace the design process, but to compress parts of it. ChatGPT helped me draft consent copy, write screen titles in plain language, and adjust legal tone for different literacy levels. Stitch helped me get from prompts to rough wireframes fast.

But both needed significant steering. ChatGPT would sometimes suggest flows that were legally unnecessary or too complex for an MVP. Stitch would give me four screens in four different visual styles - I had to manually fix spacing, component consistency, and hierarchy every time. The AI moved fast. I had to make sure it moved in the right direction.

A project like this - from research to a complete low-fidelity MVP - would normally take 4–5 weeks. With AI tools plus clear UX direction, we got there in about 1.5 weeks. The time savings weren't from cutting corners. They were from not having to start from a blank screen every time.

What we built - the CCO dashboard

The company-side product needed to give compliance teams visibility and control: incoming data requests, response workflows, consent status per user, audit trails, and reporting. The harder design problem here was learning enough about the technical side - SSO, APIs, consent lifecycle, data inventory - to design flows that were actually buildable and not just screen-deep. I had to ask better questions than I was used to asking.

What we built - the citizen app

The mobile app for Data Principals had to work for someone with low digital literacy on a slow connection, and also for someone who wanted full control over every permission they'd ever given. That range pushed us toward simplicity at every step.

A few specific decisions that shaped the experience:

  • Onboarding in plain language - "Manage consent across 100+ brands", "Raise data rights requests in a few taps", "Built for India, built for your rights." No legal preamble.
  • Unified request flow - company selection, request type, and description all on one screen instead of three separate steps
  • Requests dashboard split clearly - requests you've sent vs. requests companies have sent you (like Swiggy asking for new consent) - two completely different mental models that needed to be visually separated
  • Consent screens with clear status labels - Awaiting Response / Completed - with document upload, OTP verification, and a confirmation page that actually told you what happened next

What I had to learn to design this well

The CCO experience in particular pushed me outside my usual territory. To design flows that were technically credible, I had to get comfortable with: how databases store, access, and delete data; SSO and login flows across systems; what API-level consent syncing and revocation actually involves; the full consent lifecycle from collection through to expiry and deletion; and how data inventory and mapping works in a compliance context.

None of that was natural to me. But not knowing it would have made the design superficial. I made it part of the process - asking questions until I understood the constraints well enough to design within them, not around them.

Where it stands

Privasync is still an MVP. It hasn't been built out fully or tested at scale - some peer testing with colleagues and family helped smooth out friction, but there are no usage metrics yet. The foundation is solid. Whether it gets built further depends on Phence's roadmap and the speed at which companies start taking DPDP compliance seriously.

What I took away

AI tools are genuinely useful when you know what you're trying to achieve. When you don't, they'll confidently generate the wrong thing in a very polished way. The speed advantage is real - but only if the person using the tool is making sharp decisions about what to keep, what to cut, and what to redo from scratch.

The other thing: privacy design is not just compliance design. The moment you treat consent as a legal requirement to satisfy rather than an experience to earn, the product stops working for the person using it. Every good decision in this project came from asking whether a real person - not a DPDP clause - would understand and trust what we'd designed.

The Context

BeyondTheLoop was a European AI workspace built for teams who needed more than one model. Different tasks called for different tools. a long legal brief needed careful reasoning, a quick marketing draft needed speed, and anything touching EU customer data needed local German hosting. The product had the right answer for each situation. It just wasn't telling anyone.

When I joined in September 2024, the model selector was a plain dropdown. Five options. GPT-3.5, Mistral, Perplexity, ChatGPT-4o, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. listed with no descriptions, no guidance, and no signal about which was right for what you were doing. The product was asking users to make a consequential technical decision with zero scaffolding.

What I Needed to Understand

Before designing anything, I ran 6 short moderated sessions (30 min, remote) with existing users across different team roles to answer three questions.

Why weren't users exploring? Session recordings showed the pattern. users landed, saw the dropdown, glanced at it, and started typing. The dropdown wasn't a decision. It was a label. Nobody read it as a choice point.

What did users think the models were? The findings were consistent and humbling:

"I assumed they were the same thing with different names."

"I thought ChatGPT-4o was the best one because I'd heard of it outside the app. I never tried the others."

"I didn't know one of them stored data in Germany. That actually matters to us. we have EU clients."

Three of six users worked in roles where data residency was a genuine compliance concern. None had selected the Frankfurt-hosted Mistral model.

What did users need to know. and what could we leave out? I tested a full technical comparison table. Five of six users found it overwhelming. One said: "Now I feel like I'm going to make the wrong choice." The goal wasn't to make users knowledgeable. It was to make them confident. Those are different problems with different solutions.

How Might We

  • 1How might we help non-technical users make a meaningful model choice. without making them feel like the choice is something they could get wrong?
  • 2How might we surface BeyondTheLoop's most differentiated offering (EU data hosting on Mistral) to the users who need it. without turning the interface into a compliance checklist?
  • 3How might we give users who don't want to choose at all a path that feels considered. not like a default they got stuck with?

The Solution. LLM Choice Cards

Each model got a card. Each card had four elements, every one deliberate.

① A plain-language one-liner per model

Not a spec. Not a benchmark. A sentence a product manager could read in two seconds and act on.

  • GPT-3.5: "Fast and lightweight. Best for quick drafts, summaries, and simple tasks."
  • ChatGPT-4o: "Best for complex reasoning, long documents, and structured analysis."
  • Claude 3.5 Sonnet: "Careful, considered. Excellent at following nuanced instructions."
  • Perplexity: "Research-first. Surfaces citations alongside answers. ideal for fact-heavy work."
  • Mistral (EU Hosted): "Your data stays in Frankfurt (EU). For teams with strict data residency requirements."

② 'Best for' chips

2–3 use-case labels per card. Not exhaustive. representative. Users scan chips, not paragraphs. Examples: Long documents · Legal review · Quick drafts · Research & citations · GDPR-sensitive work

③ A trust signal on the Mistral card

A single line: "Data never leaves Frankfurt (EU)". For users with EU compliance requirements, that line was the entire reason to choose this product over US-hosted alternatives. It was buried in the old dropdown. We made it the headline of that card.

④ A "Recommended for your role" badge

The key design decision. The badge didn't say "Default". a word that implies randomness. It said "Recommended for your role". implying the system had considered something about the user specifically. It had: their account type, already stored at sign-up. Simple, honest, and enough to feel personal. Users who accepted it did so with confidence. Users who overrode it did so deliberately.

One Decision I'd Make Differently

I designed the cards for the selection screen. What I didn't redesign was the chat interface itself. Once a model was selected, it appeared only as a small label in the header. If you forgot which model you'd chosen mid-conversation, there was no ambient reminder. The cards solved the selection problem. The chat interface still treated the model as a setting, not a collaborator. That's the next problem to solve.

"We fixed the moment of choice. We didn't fix the moment of doubt. when you're four messages in and can't remember what you chose or why. That's Phase 2."

What This Was Really About

Most AI product problems are framed as capability problems. how do we make the AI smarter, faster, more accurate? This one was a trust problem. And trust problems are always about the user's internal experience, not the system's external performance.

The five models were genuinely differentiated. The product was genuinely valuable. Users just didn't trust themselves to navigate the choice. so they didn't try. The design work was mostly subtraction: removing the intimidation, removing the wrong framing, removing the feeling that there was a correct answer they might miss. What was left was five cards, ten sentences, and a badge that said we thought about you before you arrived. That was enough. It moved the number.

The Problem

BeyondTheLoop's users could switch between five AI models. GPT-3.5, Mistral, Perplexity, ChatGPT-4o, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. at any point in a conversation. The intent was flexibility. The reality was a silent failure: when a user switched models mid-chat, the new model started from nothing. No memory of the task. No memory of the context built over the last ten messages. The response was generic. The user had no idea why.

The product wasn't broken. The model switch was working exactly as designed. But the design had never told the user what they were about to lose.

Session recordings in PostHog showed a consistent failure loop: user starts a complex task on ChatGPT-4o switches to Claude mid-way Claude responds without context user re-prompts in frustration session abandoned. The switch was invisible except for a small label change in the header. The consequences didn't appear until the next message. by which point the user had already decided something was broken.

Understanding the Failure

This was a state management problem wearing a UX problem's clothes. The technical constraint was real and fixed: cross-model context passing was not on the engineering roadmap for this sprint. The design couldn't fix the underlying limitation. It had to make that limitation honest and navigable.

I watched 14 session recordings of users who had switched models mid-conversation. Three patterns emerged every time.

Pattern 1. The accidental switch

Silent switch, invisible consequence

User clicks the model selector by mistake while scrolling up to re-read the conversation. Switch happens silently. Next message gets a response that feels completely off. User assumes the AI is "having a bad day."

Pattern 2. The exploratory switch

Curiosity punished

User wants to try a different model on the same task. Switches mid-session. New model ignores all prior context and gives a generic first-response answer to a question already 8 messages deep. Frustration, not discovery.

Pattern 3. The deliberate switch

Right decision, wrong experience

User intentionally switches to Mistral for EU data compliance. New model responds correctly but without context. so the user has to re-explain everything they spent 10 minutes establishing. The switch was right. The experience was exhausting.

How Might We

  • 1How might we make context loss visible before it happens. not after the damage is done?
  • 2How might we give users a way to carry context forward. even without full cross-model memory?
  • 3How might we prevent accidental context loss without blocking users who genuinely want a fresh start?

What I Explored

Cut. blocked valid use cases

Direction A. Block mid-session switching entirely

Disable model switching once a conversation started. Show: "Start a new chat to switch models." Clean. Simple. Also completely wrong. it killed the exploratory use case driving some of the product's most engaged users.

Cut. too passive, same problem

Direction B. Inline tooltip warning

A small tooltip on hover: "Switching models will start a new context." Tested with 5 users. 4 didn't read it, all 4 were still surprised when context was lost. The warning had to be impossible to miss.

Shipped

Direction C. Modal pause with three choices

A deliberate interruption at the moment of switching. Not a blocker. a pause that forces one conscious decision. Three options: start fresh, carry a summary, or stay with the current model. Tested with 6 users. all 6 read it, all 6 made a deliberate choice. Zero unexpected context loss after shipping.

The Solution. Three Components

① The Switch Warning Modal

Triggered on any mid-session model switch after message 3. Not a blocker. a deliberate pause. Shows which model you're switching to, what you'll lose, and three clear paths forward. "Carry a summary" is visually the most prominent option. because that's what most users actually want, even if they don't know it yet.

  • "Start fresh": switch now, clean slate. For users who want a genuine reset.
  • "Carry a summary": auto-summarises the last 8 messages into a context handoff prompt, pre-loaded into the new session. (Default highlight)
  • "Stay with [current model]": escape hatch for accidental clicks. No friction.

② The Context Summary Handoff

When "Carry a summary" is selected, the system generates a 3-line plain-language summary of the session and pre-populates it as the first message in the new model's context. The user sees it before it's sent. they can edit it, trim it, or add to it. This single detail transformed the experience: users went from feeling like victims of a system limitation to feeling in deliberate control of a handoff.

③ The Persistent Model Identity Pill

A small always-visible pill in the chat header showing the current model name and a coloured dot. GPT-3.5 in green, Claude in orange, Mistral in blue, Perplexity in purple, ChatGPT-4o in teal. Solved the secondary problem of users forgetting which model they were on mid-conversation, leading to confused re-prompting.

The Detail That Changed Everything

The original spec had the summary auto-sending to the new model. no user review. Engineering preferred it. I pushed back.

"If the summary sends automatically and it's wrong, the user has no idea what the new model was told. That's worse than no handoff. it creates invisible misinformation."

Making the summary editable added one step and something much more valuable: the user's sense of authorship over the context transfer. In testing, every user who saw the editable summary felt more in control of the product than before the switch. That feeling. the system is helping me, not replacing my judgment, was the real goal.

One Decision I'd Make Differently

The modal triggers after message 3. In retrospect, that's too early. the context built by message 3 is rarely worth carrying. I'd move the trigger to message 6 or 7. The friction-to-value ratio improves significantly, and users who are genuinely mid-task are always past that point anyway.

What This Was Really About

The technical limitation. no cross-model memory. was never going to change in this sprint. The question was whether the product would pretend it didn't exist or design around it honestly. The modal, the summary handoff, and the identity pill are all the same answer: be transparent about what the system can't do, and give users the tools to work with that limitation rather than around it.

Users who understand and control their context are more confident in the product than users who never had to think about it at all. That's not a consolation prize for a missing feature. It's better product design than the feature would have been.
