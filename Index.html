About me

I look at everything like it's a design problem.
Most of the time that's a gift. Sometimes it's exhausting.

✏️

I find characters in scribbles

When I run workshops, I ask people to scribble randomly — eyes closed, no plan. Then I give them time to look at what they made and find something in it. A face. A creature. A scene. Once they see it, I ask them to bring it to life.

It's a doodling exercise I made up, but it does something real: it teaches people to look before they judge. That's the same skill that makes you a better designer, a better researcher, a better listener.

🍳

I cook with whatever's left

I don't follow recipes. I look at what's in the fridge at 9pm — half an onion, some leftover rice, a lemon going soft — and try to make something that tastes intentional.

I care about it being healthy. And I care about it not feeling like a compromise. That constraint — make something good out of what you have — is one I find genuinely enjoyable. Probably why I like design sprints.

✈️

I travel with the wrong kind of attention

I notice the queue management at a new airport before I notice the architecture. I watch how people orient themselves in a new metro system. I think about why some street food stalls have lines and others don't.

I can't turn it off. Everything I see becomes a question about why it works or doesn't. It's made me a better designer. It's also made me slightly annoying on holidays.

🤖

I build apps for my own problems

Lately, when something bothers me enough, I just build a thing. Not a prototype — an actual working app. Claude, Cursor, Gemini, Figma Make. I'm not an engineer and I don't pretend to be, but I can get from idea to working product fast enough to test whether the idea was worth having.

It's changed how I think about what "designing" means. The line between wireframe and product is thinner than it used to be.

I'm Vashishta. I studied textiles at NIFT Hyderabad, then did a Master's in Visual Communication at IIT Kanpur. I thought I'd end up in print or branding. Instead I spent the last 8 years designing apps, dashboards, and platforms.

I've worked on things used by millions of people and things used by twelve. The scale is different but the problem is always the same: does this make sense to the person using it?

I mentor at Masters' Union. Over 100 people so far. I do it because explaining design to smart people who are new to it forces me to be honest about what I actually know.

I'm based in Bengaluru. Open to design leadership roles in India, Singapore, and Southeast Asia — ideally somewhere design has a seat at the table before the product decisions are already made.

Mobile UX · Super-App Strategy · Creativeland Asia · 2019–2020

MyJio had 400M users who didn't know half the app existed

MyJio worked fine if all you wanted was to check your data balance. Jio had built a whole ecosystem of apps on top of it. Almost nobody was using them. We were brought in to figure out why, and what to do about it.

Creativeland Asia

The Brief

MyJio had one job in most users' minds: check your data, recharge if needed, close the app. Jio had built JioGames, JioCinema, JioSaavn, JioNews and more on top of it. Most users had no idea. That was the problem.

"Get people to actually use the mini-apps." — The brief, in plain terms

Business Goals

We started with a 3-hour workshop with the Jio team. Three things came out of it:

  • Get users into the mini-apps — use the homepage to surface apps they don't know about yet
  • Give people a reason to stay — the app shouldn't feel like a utility bill payment page
  • Show what Jio actually offers — especially to users considering switching networks

My Contribution

I ran the project. Set the agenda, led the workshops, did my share of the design work too. I was involved in everything from the initial audit to the final prototype.

Identifying Gaps & Opportunities

Before opening the app, we mapped where users were in their journey and what we expected to find. Then we opened the app.

Key Findings

  • Onboarding auto-rotated with no visual cue
  • Login split into multiple screens by service type
  • Navigation had 11+ items in horizontal scroll
  • Banners only showed offers, not app features
  • Mini-apps were buried in hamburger menu

Design Strategy

The redesign focused on making mini-apps discoverable and valuable through homepage visibility, improved navigation, and contextual banners that showed benefits rather than just discounts.

Best Buy · Contextual Nudges

The Situation

Push notifications are one of Best Buy's main levers for deals, offers, and service updates. The opt-in rate was at 43% with a target of 50%. We designed contextual nudges focused on high-intent moments for non-member users, who represented 70% of the opportunity.

  • Most valuable notification types: Price drops (23%), Flash sales (21%), Recommendations (20%)
  • Nudge timing mattered more than copy

  • 43% overall lift achieved
  • Best performing nudges were contextual, not generic

BRIX Dashboard · Best Buy Design Systems

What BRIX is

BRIX is the design system built by Best Buy to bring consistency across products and reduce duplicated effort. It has four libraries: BRIX Enterprise (core components), BRIX Icons, BRIX Web, and BRIX Native.

The Challenge

The system was healthy and doing real work, but nobody outside the team had visibility into its impact. Leadership needed a way to measure value, adoption, and health trends. We designed a dashboard that served two audiences with different needs.

Design Approach

  • For leadership: High-level trends, adoption rates, ROI indicators
  • For BRIX teams: Component-level detail, usage breakdown, technical debt tracking
  • Visual alignment: Dashboard designed using BRIX itself, showcasing system maturity

Key Decisions

  • Replaced static donut charts with trend-based visualizations
  • Added complexity/cost metrics for component table
  • Reorganized side sheets around browsing behavior
  • Removed old/inactive repositories from calculations

Privacy Design · AI-Powered Sprint · 2024

India passed a data privacy law. We made it something users could actually use.

Privasync is a consent management platform built for the DPDP Act — one side for everyday citizens managing their data, one side for companies handling compliance. Designed from research to MVP in about 1.5 weeks.

Why this project

India passed the Digital Personal Data Protection Act in August 2023. The law puts real accountability on companies around data collection and deletion. Unlike GDPR, it's designed for India's scale and mobile-first reality. We built Privasync to make compliance transparent and usable on both sides — for citizens and for companies.

Two Distinct Products

  • Data Principal App: Everyday citizens managing consent, tracking their data, raising requests without needing a law degree
  • CCO Dashboard: Company compliance teams receiving requests, building workflows, demonstrating compliance

Design Process

  • Used AI tools (ChatGPT, Google Stitch) strategically to compress timeline, not replace design
  • Framed AI prompts around personas for better output quality
  • Focused on plain language and low literacy accessibility
  • Built with future scalability in mind

Key Learnings

  • AI accelerates design when you know what direction to steer
  • Privacy design is about earning trust, not just meeting compliance
  • Mobile-first, context-aware design is critical for Indian users

AI Product Design · Beyond the Loop · 2024

Taking an AI workspace from MVP to something teams could actually trust.

Beyond the Loop is a European AI workspace — multi-model chat, custom assistants, hosted in Germany. They had a working MVP. I joined to figure out what was broken and help make it ready to ship.

Beyond the Loop
End-to-end product designer + dev alignment
2 months, Oct–Nov 2024
Won a 99designs contest

How it started

I entered a design contest on 99designs. The founders saw the work, liked it, and reached out. They had a team, a live product, and paying customers — but the MVP was showing its age. The brief was simple: fix what's broken and get it ready for a proper launch.

What Was Broken

  • Users picked wrong AI models and switched mid-conversation
  • Magic Write feature was buried (4-5 clicks to use)
  • Admins had no visibility into team usage or spending
  • UI was inconsistent, no shared design language

Design Solutions

  • Model Choice: Added plain-language guidance cards at conversation start
  • Magic Write: Moved inline to chat composer — 2 clicks instead of 5
  • Admin Controls: Created dashboard showing token spend, usage trends, team breakdown
  • Design System: Built lightweight component library for consistency

Results

  • 40% reduction in mid-chat model switches
  • 2.3× growth in Magic Write usage in 6 weeks
  • 30% increase in daily active teams
  • 80% admin dashboard activation rate
  • 4.3/5 NPS across pilot users

Key Takeaway

AI product design is still regular product design. The challenge is making humans feel in control, not confused or exposed. People need to understand what they're choosing before they choose it.

About me

I look at everything like it's a design problem.
Most of the time that's a gift. Sometimes it's exhausting.

✏️

I find characters in scribbles

When I run workshops, I ask people to scribble randomly — eyes closed, no plan. Then I give them time to look at what they made and find something in it. A face. A creature. A scene. Once they see it, I ask them to bring it to life.

It's a doodling exercise I made up, but it does something real: it teaches people to look before they judge. That's the same skill that makes you a better designer, a better researcher, a better listener.

🍳

I cook with whatever's left

I don't follow recipes. I look at what's in the fridge at 9pm — half an onion, some leftover rice, a lemon going soft — and try to make something that tastes intentional.

I care about it being healthy. And I care about it not feeling like a compromise. That constraint — make something good out of what you have — is one I find genuinely enjoyable. Probably why I like design sprints.

✈️

I travel with the wrong kind of attention

I notice the queue management at a new airport before I notice the architecture. I watch how people orient themselves in a new metro system. I think about why some street food stalls have lines and others don't.

I can't turn it off. Everything I see becomes a question about why it works or doesn't. It's made me a better designer. It's also made me slightly annoying on holidays.

🤖

I build apps for my own problems

Lately, when something bothers me enough, I just build a thing. Not a prototype — an actual working app. Claude, Cursor, Gemini, Figma Make. I'm not an engineer and I don't pretend to be, but I can get from idea to working product fast enough to test whether the idea was worth having.

It's changed how I think about what "designing" means. The line between wireframe and product is thinner than it used to be.

The Brief

MyJio had one job in most users' minds: check your data, recharge if needed, close the app. Jio had built JioGames, JioCinema, JioSaavn, JioNews and more on top of it. Most users had no idea. That was the problem.

"Get people to actually use the mini-apps." — The brief, in plain terms

Business Goals

We started with a 3-hour workshop with the Jio team. Three things came out of it:

  • Get users into the mini-apps — use the homepage to surface apps they don't know about yet
  • Give people a reason to stay — the app shouldn't feel like a utility bill payment page
  • Show what Jio actually offers — especially to users considering switching networks

My Contribution

I ran the project. Set the agenda, led the workshops, did my share of the design work too. I was involved in everything from the initial audit to the final prototype.

Identifying Gaps & Opportunities

Before opening the app, we mapped where users were in their journey and what we expected to find. Then we opened the app.

1. Onboarding Stage — Key Findings

  • The onboarding carousel auto-rotated with no visual cue. Most users never knew it was moving, let alone interactable.
  • Login was split into separate screens by service type — Mobile, Fiber, Fi. Confusing before you've even got in.
  • QR login was meant only for Fiber and JioLink users. It was shown to everyone. Wrong expectations every time.
  • "Not a Jio customer?" as the first message to non-Jio users — not a great welcome.

2. Home Screen & Search — Key Findings

  • First thing after login: a popup about SIM and Fiber plans. A wasted chance to show what the app could do.
  • 11+ nav items in a horizontal scroll bar at the top. Getting to page 8 required real commitment.
  • Search defaulted to "Mobile" with a dropdown to switch categories. Most users never switched it.
  • Banners were almost entirely offers and discounts. Nothing showed what you could actually do inside the mini-apps.
  • "View More" on every banner. It sounded like a redirect to the app store, not an invitation to explore.

3. Navigation & IA — Key Findings

  • The first 3 or 4 nav items got all the attention. Everything past that was invisible unless you went looking.
  • The hamburger menu and the floating "candy box" both opened the same list. Two doors, one room.
  • The search bar only searched within the menu page. Looking for JioGames through search? Dead end.

Understanding Users

We spoke to 8 MyJio users — phone calls and in-person. Quick, structured conversations focused on how they actually used the app and what they thought it was for.

How Might We…

  • 1 How do we show people what the app can do before they've decided it's just for recharges?
  • 2 How do we redesign navigation so mini-apps are found, not buried?
  • 3 How do we make the homepage feel like a place worth spending time, not just a utility screen?
  • 4 How do we interrupt the "open, recharge, close" habit — without being annoying about it?
  • 5 How do we give people a reason to come back to the mini-apps more than once?

Design Decisions & What We Learned

For each question, we ran two rounds of 10-minute rapid ideation internally. The strongest ideas went to wireframes. We had a hard deadline — the Annual Jio Conference — so we cut anything that wasn't high impact and fast to execute.

65% skipped the carousel — internal data

1. A 7-second video worked better than a 6-slide carousel

We thought a carousel gave users control — read at your own pace, skip what you don't care about. 65% of new users just skipped the whole thing. A short video covering the same ground in 7 seconds, with no skip option, kept people watching.

7/8 users felt interrupted — internal testing

2. Asking users what they liked — every single time — made them trust the app less

The logic was sound: if a user reaches the end of a content tile, they're interested. Ask them what they want more of. 7 out of 8 users found it pushy. It felt like the app was trying to profile them. We dropped the explicit prompts and shifted to watching what they actually did — scroll depth, time on tile, return visits.

5/8 iPhone users — high satisfaction with universal search

3. Universal search, accessible from anywhere, made the app feel much bigger

We put a universal search bar at the top — voice included — modelled on how Safari's address bar works. iPhone users got it immediately. Trending content from across the mini-apps surfaced upfront, so even people who didn't know what they were looking for started finding things. Android users were equally comfortable with it.

Each mini-app got a widget with 3 CTAs — the most common things users do inside it. Jio picked the initial order based on business priority. Over time, the order would shift based on what each user actually used. Three actions per widget was a deliberate limit — more than that and nobody reads any of them.

5. Banners that felt personal kept people coming back for 30 days

We stopped treating banner space as a billboard for offers. Instead, banners surfaced the mini-apps each user had already shown interest in. 32% of daily active users who tapped a banner were still using mini-apps 30 days later. That's not a conversion stat — that's a habit forming.

65% rise in daily active user clicks — Jio data

6. Surfacing the right mini-app at the right position in the grid made a measurable difference

The quick-access grid on the homepage reordered itself based on what each user actually opened. Apps the user rarely touched got a "Promoted" label to nudge them into trying it. Apps moved up in the grid got 65% more daily active user clicks than when they were buried in the hamburger menu.

7. A five-stage notification strategy — not tested, but worth noting

We designed notifications around triggers, actions, and variable rewards — five stages, one per mini-app. Jio wanted to focus on the homepage first, so this didn't get tested. It's still sitting there as a next-phase opportunity that I think could meaningfully extend what the homepage changes started.

Key Learnings

  • If users have decided your app does one thing, they'll never look for the other things — even if they're right there. Onboarding is the only reliable window to reframe that expectation.
  • Mapping the funnel before the audit meant we weren't just finding problems — we were finding the right problems.
  • A redesign that ships all at once is a redesign that gets blamed for every user complaint. We pushed for staged rollouts. Jio agreed. It helped.

Best Buy · Contextual Nudges

The situation

Push notifications are one of Best Buy's main levers for deals, offers, and service updates — directly tied to revenue and retention. By the end of 2024, opt-in rate was at 43%. The FY2025 target was 50%. Not a massive gap on paper, but 7 percentage points across a user base of that size is a lot of people.

The app already had nudges in 7 places. Only three of them — positions 1, 3, and 6 — were driving almost all of the opt-ins. Previous attempts to improve the other placements had consistently failed. The brief from Product: find new, high-intent, contextual moments to ask — especially for non-member users — without compromising privacy or user trust.

My role was end-to-end: framing the problem, leading discovery, defining the nudge strategy, and designing the experience. Product and Analytics were in the room for data decisions and feasibility, but the UX direction was mine to own.

Why non-members

The baseline data revealed a clear segmentation gap. Members opted in at roughly 3x the rate of non-members. But this wasn't a user demand problem — it was a design and messaging problem. Members had already been primed through loyalty context and prior app engagement. Non-members were getting the same generic nudge with zero personal relevance.

Non-members were also the bigger opportunity by far: they represented around 70% of the eligible user base but only 13% of opt-ins. A focused 10-point lift in non-member opt-in rate — from 27% to 37% — would alone contribute 5 to 6 points toward the 50% target. That's where we focused.

An in-app survey of non-members added another layer. When asked what types of notifications they'd actually want:

  • Saved Items Price Drop Notifications — 23%
  • Flash Sale or Limited-Time Offer Alerts — 21%
  • Gift Recommendations — 20.5%
  • Customer Service Updates — 18%
  • Personalised Shopping Suggestions — 7%
  • New Product Launch Alerts — 5%

Price drops and sale alerts were clearly what non-members cared about. That shaped the copy strategy across every nudge we designed — lead with the specific value, not a generic "allow notifications" ask.

The Saved Items story

This is the one I want to spend the most time on — not because it worked, but because of what happened when it didn't.

The "Saved Items & Lists" page looked like a promising nudge location. Users had already shown intent by saving something. Our survey data backed it up — price drop alerts were the top thing non-members said they'd want. On the surface, the case seemed obvious.

But when I looked at the actual conversion data, one number stood out: only 0.6% of users who added items to their cart from the Saved Items page went on to purchase. That's not a healthy funnel. Adding a nudge that encouraged users to hold off and wait for a price drop — which is what Product was proposing — risked making that number worse, not better.

My concern with the product team's proposal

The proposal was to show a nudge when users landed on the Saved Items page, prompting them to enable price drop alerts instead of removing items from their list. The logic made sense at a surface level. But I was worried we'd be actively interrupting users at a moment of purchase consideration and redirecting their attention toward a future event — which, given the existing 0.6% conversion rate, felt like pouring more water into a leaking bucket.

I pushed back. Not against nudging on this page entirely, but against nudging at the wrong moment within it.

What I proposed instead

Two more contextual triggers — both of which respond to something the user is actually doing rather than just landing on a page:

  • Nudge when the user clicks "Edit" or tries to remove an item — that's the moment of highest relevance. "Don't remove this — we'll let you know if the price drops." The user is about to lose the item. The value of the notification is immediately obvious.
  • Nudge when the user tries to exit the page — a soft exit-intent prompt, not an aggressive modal. "Your saved items will still be here. We'll notify you if anything changes in price." Low interruption, high relevance.

What actually shipped — and what happened

Despite the data and the pushback, Product moved forward with the on-load nudge on the Saved Items page. Their reasoning had more to do with roadmap momentum than user behaviour. The nudge went live.

By the end of Q1 2025, it had added roughly 5,000 new opt-ins — about 0.1%. And the 0.6% purchase conversion rate had dropped to 0.3%. Exactly what we were worried about. The business team pulled the nudge.

We're now pushing for the edit-intent and exit-intent patterns to be added back to the roadmap. They haven't shipped yet.

PDP to GHP — what went live and worked

While exploring the Product Detail Page, we found a behaviour pattern worth acting on. After a user saves a product on the PDP and exits to the homepage, they're essentially done exploring for now — the homepage is their resting point. We confirmed this with CJA data: around 2 lakh clicks on the PDP-to-home path were ending the session, not continuing it.

That exit moment turned out to be a good place to ask. The user had just expressed clear intent — saved a product — and was pausing. A bottom drawer nudge offering deal alerts for that specific product felt timely, not intrusive. We A/B tested two variants: one with a "Not now" button, one with just a close icon in the corner. The close icon variant performed better — around 4% increase in overall opt-ins, adding roughly 3,000 new users in the first month. This one is live.

One thing we learned during testing: animated nudges, while visually interesting, added cognitive load at the wrong moment. A user who hasn't opted in yet is already evaluating whether to trust the ask — layering an animation on top of that made them hesitate, not engage. We kept the final design clean and static.

Sale event nudges — a glimpse

Seasonal sale events — Black Friday, Back to School, Christmas — see some of the highest app traffic of the year, with a significant portion of those users having push notifications disabled. We worked on a GHP-level nudge strategy for these events: a global opt-in for sale alerts plus a product-specific SKU carousel for users who wanted to track specific items. Both approaches were needed because a global nudge alone would miss users who didn't see anything they wanted, and a product-specific one would miss casual browsers. This work is still in progress.

What I took away

The Saved Items story is the most useful thing in this case study — not because it's a win, but because it shows what happens when design intuition backed by data gets overruled by roadmap pressure. The nudge failed in exactly the way we said it would. That's not a satisfying outcome, but it's an honest one.

The bigger lesson: document everything when you have a clear, data-backed position. Not to say "I told you so" later — but because a documented hypothesis gives you the foundation to re-enter the conversation when things go wrong. That's how the edit-intent pattern is getting back on the roadmap now.

BRIX is the design system built by Best Buy to bring consistency across products and reduce duplicated effort between designers and developers. With that investment came accountability. Every quarter, the BRIX team needed to show the value it was adding to the business. For a while, the engineering manager had built a small internal tool that pulled from Excel sheets and generated downloadable reports. That worked — until the system started scaling fast enough that the business wanted something more visible and ongoing. That's what gave this project its start.

Where I came in

Suyash and Shivani had been leading the project for three weeks when I joined. By that point there was already an information architecture and a first draft of wireframes. My role was to come in, absorb the context quickly, and help push the design from a functional skeleton to something that actually communicated the system's value clearly — both visually and structurally.

Getting up to speed fast was its own challenge. I was new to the organisation, the BRIX team had their own terminology (much of it engineering-side), and there were tracking gaps on the design side — component reuse in Figma wasn't being measured the same way as code-side adoption. Getting everyone to agree on a shared language before designing anything was necessary and took time.

What we were designing for

Two types of users, one dashboard. Top management needed a summary — adoption trends, ROI, system health at a glance. The BRIX Engineering and Design Managers needed something more detailed — which components had debt, where legacy usage was still high, where the team should focus next.

We aligned early on three design principles that shaped every decision:

  • Content personalisation — surface the right level of detail to the right person
  • Better data visualisation — replace static numbers with visuals that actually told a story
  • Visual alignment with BRIX — the dashboard should look like the system it was measuring

Ideation and early feedback

Time overlap between team members was limited — we were working across different schedules — so we ran structured feedback calls instead of in-person workshops. That constraint actually helped. Having to present direction on a call forced us to be specific about what we were proposing and why. The feedback we got early shaped a lot of the structure: unify the terminology around "BRIX Enterprise" rather than calling it "new" vs "old"; lead with positives for top management and keep the remediation detail for the operational layer; stop showing numbers that duplicated each other without adding insight.

The design decisions that mattered

A few specific calls are worth naming because they changed the feel of the dashboard significantly:

  • The circular volume visualisation for adoption rate was removed. It looked sophisticated but it didn't communicate change over time. A static donut chart tells you where you are — it doesn't show momentum. We replaced it with a trend-based view that made progress visible at a glance.
  • The component table got richer. Not all components take the same time and effort to build — a complex data table is a very different investment than a button. We added a complexity and cost-to-build column so that leadership could see where the team was actually spending its time, not just count components as if they were equivalent units.
  • Side sheets were restructured around browsing behaviour. The original layout put information in the order it was easiest to pull from the data, not the order a person would want to read it. We reorganised them so the most decision-relevant information came first, with supporting detail further down.

Privacy Design · AI-Powered Sprint · 2024

India passed a data privacy law. We made it something users could actually use.

Privasync is a consent management platform built for the DPDP Act — one side for everyday citizens managing their data, one side for companies handling compliance. Designed from research to MVP in about 1.5 weeks, leaning hard into AI tools while staying in the driver's seat throughout.

Why this project

In August 2023, India passed the Digital Personal Data Protection Act — a defining law that puts real accountability on companies around how they collect, store, and delete personal data. Unlike GDPR or CCPA, the DPDP Act is designed for India's scale: hundreds of millions of mobile-first users with widely varying digital literacy levels. The law isn't just asking for better privacy policies. It's asking for tools that make compliance transparent and usable on both sides — for the person whose data it is, and for the company responsible for it.

When MeitY announced a design challenge around the Act, Phence Private Limited asked me to lead the design of Privasync — a two-sided consent management platform responding directly to what the law requires.

This project was close to me personally. Privacy and data control are things I care about. Getting a chance to design a product that treated consent as an experience rather than a checkbox felt worth doing properly.

What Privasync needed to be

The law created two distinct users who needed completely different products:

  • Data Principals — everyday citizens who want to see which companies have their data, manage their consent, and raise access or deletion requests without needing a law degree to do it
  • Chief Compliance Officers (CCOs) and privacy teams — company-side roles responsible for receiving and responding to those requests, building automated workflows, and demonstrating compliance to regulators

The key highlights of the Act that shaped almost every design decision: informed consent is mandatory before any personal data is processed; every Data Principal has the right to access, correct, delete, or restrict their data; companies must respond to requests within defined timelines; non-compliance can lead to fines of up to ₹250 crore or more.

Who helped shape it

I wasn't working alone. Varun Thopucherla (CEO) and Amarnath (CTO) at Phence gave me consistent feedback from a non-designer, operations, and technical implementation lens. They helped validate user flows, simplify things I'd over-engineered, and keep the design grounded in what could actually be built. That kind of input — from people who think about systems rather than screens — made a real difference to the quality of what we produced.

Research and personas

I started by looking at existing tools — Transcend.io, Ketch — and they were well-made but built for GDPR or CCPA. The assumptions baked into them didn't translate to India's context: the language, the scale, the mobile-first reality, the varying literacy levels. So I set those aside and mapped from the brief outward.

Two personas, built from the legal requirements and validated with the Phence team. I used them actively throughout — not just as research artefacts, but as prompts when working with AI tools. When I needed to write consent summary screens or adjust tone on legal copy, I framed the prompt around the persona first.

How AI changed my process

This was the first project where I leaned heavily into ChatGPT and Google Stitch — not to replace the design process, but to compress parts of it. ChatGPT helped me draft consent copy, write screen titles in plain language, and adjust legal tone for different literacy levels. Stitch helped me get from prompts to rough wireframes fast.

But both needed significant steering. ChatGPT would sometimes suggest flows that were legally unnecessary or too complex for an MVP. Stitch would give me four screens in four different visual styles — I had to manually fix spacing, component consistency, and hierarchy every time. The AI moved fast. I had to make sure it moved in the right direction.

A project like this — from research to a complete low-fidelity MVP — would normally take 4–5 weeks. With AI tools plus clear UX direction, we got there in about 1.5 weeks. The time savings weren't from cutting corners. They were from not having to start from a blank screen every time.

What we built — the CCO dashboard

The company-side product needed to give compliance teams visibility and control: incoming data requests, response workflows, consent status per user, audit trails, and reporting. The harder design problem here was learning enough about the technical side — SSO, APIs, consent lifecycle, data inventory — to design flows that were actually buildable and not just screen-deep. I had to ask better questions than I was used to asking.

What we built — the citizen app

The mobile app for Data Principals had to work for someone with low digital literacy on a slow connection, and also for someone who wanted full control over every permission they'd ever given. That range pushed us toward simplicity at every step.

A few specific decisions that shaped the experience:

  • Onboarding in plain language — "Manage consent across 100+ brands", "Raise data rights requests in a few taps", "Built for India, built for your rights." No legal preamble.
  • Unified request flow — company selection, request type, and description all on one screen instead of three separate steps
  • Requests dashboard split clearly — requests you've sent vs. requests companies have sent you (like Swiggy asking for new consent) — two completely different mental models that needed to be visually separated
  • Consent screens with clear status labels — Awaiting Response / Completed — with document upload, OTP verification, and a confirmation page that actually told you what happened next

What I had to learn to design this well

The CCO experience in particular pushed me outside my usual territory. To design flows that were technically credible, I had to get comfortable with: how databases store, access, and delete data; SSO and login flows across systems; what API-level consent syncing and revocation actually involves; the full consent lifecycle from collection through to expiry and deletion; and how data inventory and mapping works in a compliance context.

None of that was natural to me. But not knowing it would have made the design superficial. I made it part of the process — asking questions until I understood the constraints well enough to design within them, not around them.

Where it stands

Privasync is still an MVP. It hasn't been built out fully or tested at scale — some peer testing with colleagues and family helped smooth out friction, but there are no usage metrics yet. The foundation is solid. Whether it gets built further depends on Phence's roadmap and the speed at which companies start taking DPDP compliance seriously.

What I took away

AI tools are genuinely useful when you know what you're trying to achieve. When you don't, they'll confidently generate the wrong thing in a very polished way. The speed advantage is real — but only if the person using the tool is making sharp decisions about what to keep, what to cut, and what to redo from scratch.

The other thing: privacy design is not just compliance design. The moment you treat consent as a legal requirement to satisfy rather than an experience to earn, the product stops working for the person using it. Every good decision in this project came from asking whether a real person — not a DPDP clause — would understand and trust what we'd designed.

How it started

I entered a design contest on 99designs. The founders saw the work, liked it, and reached out. They had a team, a live product, and paying customers — but the MVP was showing its age. What we were working with

The existing product had the right ideas — multi-model chat, team admin controls, EU data hosting. But the experience had a few real problems:

  • Users kept choosing the wrong AI model for the task, then switching mid-conversation
  • Magic Write — one of the most useful features — required 4–5 clicks and a detour through a sidebar. Most users never found it
  • Admins had no clear view of what their teams were doing or how much they were spending
  • The UI was inconsistent — cobbled together from a partial local design system with no shared language between designers and devs

The product wasn't broken. It just required too much patience from users who didn't yet know why they should bother.

Who we were designing for

We didn't start from scratch with personas. The business had already spoken to early customers — European startups and mid-size companies. I reverse-engineered three types from those conversations:

  • Managers and business owners — they wanted to know what was happening, what it cost, and whether their data was safe
  • Knowledge workers and team leads — they wanted to get work done fast without fighting the interface
  • Heavy users and analysts — they wanted to explore different models for different tasks and compare outputs

The European angle mattered. Data hosted in Germany was a genuine differentiator against US competitors. Users needed to trust the product with company data — so trust signals had to be visible, not just in the marketing copy but in the product itself.

Navigation — making the product make sense

The existing nav mixed admin settings with daily workflows in no particular order. People couldn't tell where anything lived. We restructured it into three clear areas: chat and tools, settings, and a persistent sidebar for quick access to pinned assistants and recent conversations. The goal was that a new user could orient themselves in 30 seconds without reading any documentation.

Model choice — stop making users guess

People were picking models without knowing what they were for. So we explained the difference in plain language. At the start of a conversation, a set of model cards show each option with a one-line description — "Good for drafting," "Better for analysis," "Fast and lightweight." No jargon, no parameters. And if you change your mind mid-conversation, you can switch models from a dropdown without losing the thread.

Four to five clicks, minimum. After the redesign, it's inline in the chat composer.
